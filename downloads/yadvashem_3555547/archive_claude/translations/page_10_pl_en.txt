Only later on the road, in a certain prison during a rest stop, was I unchained and shackled again with another prisoner, one Jan Żawadzki. We were pushed back in the direction of Krasnystaw. In Krasnystaw we stopped at the local prison. The prison warden took pity on us and ordered soup to be cooked for us. They unchained us and placed us in cells. At night at 2 o'clock a bomb fell on one of the corners of the prison. The guards, frightened, left us locked in the cells and fled, taking our personal belongings on carts. In the morning - seeing that there was no prison administration, we began to break down the doors. The doors of my cell were broken down first. I found tools and decided to break down all the doors. We were free, but still locked in the prison courtyard. I made a human ladder, we got onto the wall and began calling for help. The local population came to our aid, broke down the main gate and we were finally truly free. Now we divided into small groups and set out on the road. My travel companion was quite characteristic. He was a Jew, a cobbler by trade. He was sentenced to 15 years in prison for the following incident: his daughter had an illegitimate child with a Pole who abandoned her. The grandfather wanted to make a Jew of his grandson, subjected him to circumcision - then the child died. For this crime he received his sentence and like me, the war freed him. We headed toward Lwów. Having about 5-6 km. before Lwów, Soviet soldiers stopped me and took me to headquarters as a suspicious person without any documents. In addition, they saw on me the prison garb of Świętokrzyskie prison. After long investigations I was released. During this time the war campaign in Poland ended and the Soviet Army occupied the territories of Western Ukraine and Belarus. When the Germans withdrew from Borysław, I traveled to my native city. I decided to volunteer for the Soviet Army. I treated this step as a continuation of the prewar struggle against nationalism.
